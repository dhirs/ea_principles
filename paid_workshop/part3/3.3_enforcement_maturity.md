# Part 3.3 — Enforcement Maturity: Attest → Verify → Provide

**Maven one-liner:** A built standard is still a wish until something stops non-compliant work from shipping — enforcement comes in three graded strengths, applied per control, not org-wide.

## The point of this subpart

You've prioritised (3.1) and built (3.2). But a standard with no enforcement is a wish with a signature. This subpart is the operating model that makes standards stick — and because no organisation can start at full rigour, enforcement is **graded by maturity** and applied **per control**, not as a single org-wide setting.

## The core principle

> An EA gate consumes machine-generated evidence or enforces a non-bypassable control. It never consumes self-attestation as proof.

Real orgs can't begin there, so grade enforcement along three rungs and apply the right rung **per control**.

## The three rungs

| Level | Strength | What it is | Who produces the evidence |
|---|---|---|---|
| **1** | **Attest** | Project self-certifies coverage in a guardrail YAML — every threat dispositioned, named owner, signed at the ARB | the project (declaration) |
| **2** | **Verify** | EA fires an adversarial corpus at the *running* system and scores it — the project can't fake it | EA (measurement) |
| **3** | **Provide** | EA owns the control as non-bypassable infra — a central SDK/gateway does the filtering, scanning, logging | the platform (provision) |

**Honest limits to teach:**

- **L1 (Attest)** proves a team *thought about* and *claimed* a control — never that the claim is true. Its teeth come from the named sign-off wired to post-incident accountability, plus a CI check that every threat is dispositioned (completeness, not efficacy).
- **L2 (Verify)** only works for **probe-testable** controls (injection, jailbreak, toxicity, grounding). Structural/process controls (logging completeness, provenance, human oversight) are not probe-testable and stay at L1 or move to L3.
- **L3 (Provide)** — *an SDK alone is NOT L3.* A library is opt-in; if a project can still call the model directly, L3 collapses to L1. L3 = SDK **+ platform-enforced egress routing**, or it isn't L3. And the gateway only owns horizontal controls (filtering, logging, tool-auth) — domain logic ("refund ≤ £X") stays with the project.

## Two cross-cutting rails

- **CI (deploy-time, every level).** Not a level — the rail present at all of them, escalating with the rung: L1 = YAML complete; L2 = harness passes (0 overrides); L3 = routing proof + signed attestation + build provenance. No green, no deploy.
- **Platform policy (runtime).** CI is deploy-time; a team can pass then reconfigure. L3's non-bypassability lives at runtime — egress policy + telemetry. So the enforcement plane is always **CI (deploy) + platform policy (runtime), paired.**

## Five rules so the model survives reality

1. **Levels coexist; they don't replace.** Pick the rung per control; some controls stay at L1 permanently.
2. **An SDK is not L3 without egress enforcement.** The control is the routing policy.
3. **L1 must machine-check completeness and wire to accountability** — or it's theatre.
4. **Maturity is a per-concern portfolio, not an org-wide scalar.** You'll be L3 on prompt-security and L1 on fairness. Report the distribution.
5. **CI (deploy) + platform policy (runtime), always paired.** Neither alone holds the line.

## Takeaway for this subpart

> Enforcement isn't binary. Grade it — attest, verify, provide — and apply the right rung per control, riding CI at deploy and platform policy at runtime. Self-cert wired to accountability; the SDK wired to egress enforcement; or neither earns its level.
