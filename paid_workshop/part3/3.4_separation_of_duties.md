# Part 3.4 — Separation of Duties: Four Owners, On Purpose

**Maven one-liner:** Define, build, operate, and judge are four different owners by design — if the team that runs a check also rules on its result, the independence that makes the gate worth anything is gone.

## The point of this subpart

Enforcement (3.3) only has teeth if no single team can mark its own homework. This subpart assigns ownership: across every enforcement level, **who defines, who builds, who operates, and who judges** — and why keeping those four apart is the whole point.

## The four functions

- **EA — Lead AI Architect (DEFINE)** — owns *what a standard says* and *what "pass" means*: the criteria, thresholds, which controls are probe-testable. Defines; does not build or run.
- **EA — Governance / ARB (JUDGE)** — owns the *verdict and the gate*. Consumes evidence, certifies pass/fail, blocks release, holds waiver authority. Independent — reports to the CRO, not to delivery.
- **Engineering (BUILD)** — *builds* the platform tooling: SDK, harness, corpus framework, scorer. Builds; does not run it against projects or judge results.
- **Product (OPERATE)** — *runs* the capability as a service with an SLA: scheduling, turnaround, adoption, enablement, cost pool, reporting. Runs the machine; does not own the verdict.
- **Project team (SURFACE + REMEDIATE)** — provides the workload-specific surface (endpoint, contracts, invariants, adapter) and fixes failures.

> The governing rule: **build / operate / define / judge are four different owners, on purpose.** If the group that runs a check also rules on its result, the independence that makes the gate worth anything is gone.

## How the duties play out per level

| Function | Level 1 — Attest | Level 2 — Verify | Level 3 — Provide |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Architect (define)** | Owns the threat taxonomy + standards; sets applicability metadata that filters the list | Defines "pass": threshold, what counts as an override, which controls are probe-testable | Owns what the SDK/gateway must enforce and the routing policy's required behaviour |
| **Governance/ARB (judge)** | Runs the ARB review; challenges the N/A and accepted dispositions; certifies; wires to post-incident | Owns the verdict + gate; consumes the report; certifies; blocks release | Gate = routing proof + provenance + signed attestation; owns waivers for genuine gaps |
| **Engineering (build)** | Minimal — optionally a ~20-line completeness linter | Builds the harness, corpus library + generator, scoring engine; keeps the corpus current | Builds + maintains the SDK, gateway, egress/IAM enforcement, migration tooling |
| **Product (operate)** | Owns the attestation template + submission process; tracks which workloads are signed | Runs it as a service: schedules runs, SLA, cost pool, coverage reporting | Operates the gateway: SLA, adoption + brownfield migration, FinOps, enable-before-enforce |
| **Project (surface)** | Authors the YAML, dispositions every relevant standard, names the owner, signs | Staging endpoint + creds, the invariants/oracle, a ~15-line adapter; fixes fails | Routes all model calls through the sanctioned client; keeps domain controls; migrates legacy calls |

## Takeaway for this subpart — and the close of Part 3

> Architecture defines the bar, Engineering builds the machine, Product runs it, Governance owns the verdict — four owners, on purpose. Enterprise AI fails when those collapse into one team, or when no one commits past Level 1. The catalogue was never the hard part; this climb is.
