# Part 2.2 — Anchoring Legal & Risk: Two Ways In

**Maven one-liner:** Two ways into the catalogue — start from regulation (EU AI Act → obligations) or start from your own Risk & Governance team's priorities.

## The point of this subpart

Once you've decided Legal & Risk is where you start (2.1), you face a choice: **where do the obligations come from?** There is no single correct anchor. Two routes work, and which one you take depends on where the organisation's authority and urgency already sit.

## Route A — Regulation-first

Start from the binding instrument and work down. This is how our own architecture is built: take the **EU AI Act**, identify which obligations are system-level and GenAI-distinctive, and convert each into a concrete, testable standard.

- **Strength:** comprehensive and defensible — you can trace every standard back to a legal article.
- **Best when:** the organisation operates under a clear regulatory regime, or needs an auditable line from law to control.
- **The mechanics** (drop org-level obligations, keep system-level, filter to GenAI-distinctive, then decompose → crosswalk → write) are detailed in `part_2.md`.

## Route B — Governance-first

Start from your own **Risk / Governance team** and build only the standards they already care about. Instead of deriving obligations top-down from a statute, you take their live concern list — the risks they're already accountable for — and turn those into standards.

- **Strength:** fast, organisationally aligned, and pre-sponsored — you're building what someone with authority already wants enforced.
- **Best when:** Governance has a mature risk register, or you need early wins and adoption momentum rather than exhaustive coverage.

## The one rule that holds across both routes

Whichever anchor you choose, the **ownership boundary is the same**:

- **Governance / Risk owns the obligation set** — *what* must be met (the legal interpretation).
- **Enterprise Architecture owns the conversion** — turning each obligation into an implementable, testable standard (*how* it's built and verified).

EA does not invent the legal baseline; Governance does not write the engineering controls. The obligation set is the input EA receives — and EA's job starts when it arrives. (Full hand-off model and the EU-AI-Act / standard / ISO 42001 three-layer distinction are in `part_2.md`.)

## Takeaway for this subpart

> There's no single correct starting point for Legal & Risk. Anchor regulation-first for coverage and traceability, or governance-first for speed and sponsorship — but either way, Governance owns the "what" and EA owns the "how."
