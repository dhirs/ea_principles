# Part 2.3 — What Earns a Place in the Catalogue

**Maven one-liner:** The two-part test every best practice must pass — enforceable and adoptable — and why a good practice that fails it stays guidance, not a standard.

## The point of this subpart

Sourcing (2.2) gives you *candidates*, not standards. A best practice is just a good idea until it earns a place in the catalogue. This subpart is the gate: **the criteria that decide which candidates become standards — and which don't.** The discipline here is what keeps the catalogue small, enforceable, and actually adopted instead of bloated and ignored.

## The two-part test

A best practice becomes a standard only when it passes **both** tests:

### a) It is enforceable

There must be an **objective gate** — a pass/fail test you can actually run, in CI or at the ARB. If you can't prove compliance with a check, it isn't a standard; it's a hope. This is the non-negotiable: **no gate, no standard — just a wish.** A standard must carry a gate (the test) and evidence (what proves it).

### b) It is adoptable — the EA's judgement call

Even an enforceable best practice can fail here. The EA makes a deliberate judgement: **is this so complex that it will be rejected, waived, or quietly ignored?** A control that's technically correct but so heavy that teams route around it doesn't protect anything — it just generates waivers. If the EA is confident the cost of compliance will get it thrown out, it doesn't belong in the catalogue *as a mandated standard* (yet). Adoption is a design constraint, not an afterthought.

This is judgement, not a formula — but it's the EA's judgement to make, and making it deliberately is the job.

## Example — a best practice that should NOT become a standard

Take **"keep prompts clear and on-brand."** It's a genuinely good practice and it serves CX. But:

- **Enforceable?** No. There's no objective gate — "clear" and "on-brand" are subjective, and you can't write a deterministic pass/fail check for them.
- **Result:** it fails test (a) outright. It stays **guidance** — documented, encouraged, reviewed by humans — but it never becomes a gated standard, because mandating an unverifiable rule just creates arguments at the ARB.

The lesson: not everything good is a standard. The catalogue is reserved for the practices you can *prove* and teams will actually *adopt*. Everything else lives as guidance.

## Takeaway for this subpart — and the close of Part 2

> A catalogue's value is in what you leave out. A best practice becomes a standard only when it's both **enforceable** (an objective gate) and **adoptable** (not so complex it gets rejected). A good practice that fails either test stays guidance — anchored to what the business is liable for, not to the completeness of any framework.
