Who does what

Reports to Steering Committee

Head of AI CoE

  • Takes the budget from the CIO and turns it into a funded, staffed capability.
  • Defends the catalogue's funding; answers to the Steering Committee.
  • Does not write code or author standards.
Accountable for: the CoE delivering value for the money.
Direct report · the WHAT

Lead AI Architect

  • Owns the catalogue: principles, standards, sized blueprints.
  • Owns the architecture discipline — every pod architect (solid line), deployed into pods (dotted).
  • Owns technical correctness and cross-catalogue consistency.
Accountable for: is each standard right, and do they hang together?
Direct report · the BUILD

Head of Engineering

  • Runs the pods that build standards into reference implementations.
  • Project-shaped work: build, ship, disband, move on.
  • POD Lead PMs report up to him (via a Head of PM only at 5+ pods).
Accountable for: shipping the quarter's builds.
Direct report · the RUN

Head of Product

  • Owns every live standard as a product: versioning, deprecation, the SLA.
  • Owns run / platform engineers (on-call) and adoption / enablement.
  • Owns the consumer relationship with project teams.
Accountable for: live standards holding up and getting used.
Under Engineering

The POD

  • POD Lead (PM) leads delivery; ~2 pillars' worth of work, re-pointed each quarter.
  • One architect (dotted line to the Lead AI Architect) owns the standard's content.
  • 2–3 developers + platform engineers build it.
Accountable for: one standard, end to end, to the readiness gate.
Under Product

Run, Platform & Enablement

  • Run / platform engineers keep the central SDK alive and on-call to the SLA.
  • Adoption / enablement embeds with project teams for the first weeks.
  • Goal: a self-service paved road, not permanent hand-holding.
Accountable for: uptime, support, and real adoption.

Two seams that make it hold

Architects belong to the architecture line

Pod architects report solid-line to the Lead AI Architect and are only deployed (dotted) into pods. If they reported to the pod instead, the catalogue diverges within two quarters — different conventions, different schemas, different ideas of "done." The dotted/solid split is what keeps it one product.

Build → Run handoff gate

Engineering builds; Product owns the SLA — so Product can't inherit something it can't operate. A standard isn't "done" until Product accepts it against a readiness checklist (observability wired, runbook exists, on-call path defined). Without the gate you get throw-it-over-the-wall.

Grow-into, not launch:

• Head of PM — only at ~5+ pods. Below that, POD Lead PMs report straight to the Head of Engineering; a PMO layer is pure overhead.

• Governance / Risk — borrowed (dotted) from existing compliance & EA at the start, not a dedicated hire. Runs the ARB and the audit trail.

• Splitting platform out of delivery — only once the live catalogue's maintenance load starts starving new builds (~15–20 live standards). Before that, one team.
